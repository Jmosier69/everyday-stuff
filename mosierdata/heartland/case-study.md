# Case Study: Heartland for Children

**Client:** Heartland for Children, Lakeland, Florida
**Systems built:** Rudolph Roundup (donor/child matching) + OCS/ICPC (out-of-county and interstate case tracking)
**Timeline:** 2022–2024, two separate engagements

---

## The problem

Heartland for Children is the lead community-based care agency for Polk, Highlands, and Hardee counties. They serve over 1,500 children at any given time. Kids who've been removed from their homes due to abuse, neglect, or abandonment. Kids placed with relatives, non-relatives, foster families, group homes. Kids aging out of the system and entering independent living.

They've been doing this work since 2003. And up until 2022, they were running two of their most critical operations on a Microsoft Access database that, as their director of program operations put it, was "literally standing on one leg."

Only one person could be in the system at a time. It would lock up. The coding was old enough that Microsoft no longer supported it. They were at real risk of data loss.

Everything else ran on spreadsheets. Manual tracking. Multiple spreadsheets that had to be crosswalked against each other. Reporting that was so labor-intensive they simply couldn't get to most of it.

Here's the thing about a child welfare agency running on spreadsheets: the reporting isn't optional. The Department of Children and Families requires weekly, monthly, and quarterly reports. Thirty-day status updates on every case. Notifications when deadlines are approaching. When you're tracking all of that manually across three counties, you're not doing case management. You're putting out fires.

Their words: "You really couldn't track well. We have a service out. Was someone assigned? Were they not assigned yet? Because of the volume of services, it made it even harder. You just didn't have the manpower to be able to track those things."

---

## What we built

Two systems, about a year apart.

**Rudolph Roundup** came first. It's Heartland's signature annual event: matching over 1,500 foster children with donors who fulfill their Christmas wish lists. Twenty-one years running. Before we touched it, the whole operation lived in that Access database and a pile of manual processes. Donor information in one place. Child wish lists somewhere else. The matching itself was all done by hand.

We built a web-based system that captures the entire process. Every child in foster care with a wish list goes into the system. Every donor goes into the system. And the system matches donors to children, tracks which bags have been filled, which have been picked up, and where things stand at any moment. All visible on a dashboard without running a single report.

**OCS/ICPC** came second. They came back to us because of how the first project went. This system tracks out-of-county services and interstate compact placements: children placed across county or state lines, home studies that need to happen in other jurisdictions, case plan assistance for parents in other counties. The policy requirements here are heavy. Thirty-day progress updates. Notifications. Home study tracking. Each service falls into one of about 20 different categories, and at any given moment, Heartland needs to know exactly how many children and families are affected in every single one of those categories.

The system handles all of it. Assignments, notifications, progress tracking, reporting. And like Rudolph, the dashboard gives them a real-time view of everything without touching a report.

If a question came up later, a phone call or email resolved it.

## What changed

The numbers first, because they're the kind you can take to a board:

- **15% reduction** in open OCS/ICPC services. Once everything was in the system and they could actually see what was assigned and what wasn't, they eliminated the waste.
- **Home studies completed in roughly half the time.** The system tracks studies happening in other counties and states, pushes notifications, and lets them change notification dates on the fly. Permanency moved faster because the bottleneck became visible.
- **Rudolph Roundup would take triple the man hours** to run without the system. And the program has grown significantly over the last four to five years. More donors. More children. More young adults in the independent living program. The system scaled with it.
- **Reporting went from impossible to routine.** Before, they couldn't get to it. Now they run monthly reports, weekly reports, and can pull data on anything at any time. As their data-minded director put it: "I know the data is very accurate in there."

Then the operational side, which matters just as much:

Their director's favorite feature on both systems is the dashboard. For Rudolph, when volunteers are coming in and they have 1,500 children to serve before Christmas, they can see at a glance how many bags are filled and how many are left. No reports. No spreadsheets. Just look at the screen.

For OCS/ICPC, same thing. Twenty categories of services, and at any moment they know exactly how many they have in each one, how many children affected, how many families affected. Their words: "That has been a huge time saver."

## The pattern

Heartland for Children isn't unique in having outgrown their systems. They're unique in having done something about it before the Access database finally gave out.

Most organizations in their position keep patching. They add another spreadsheet. They hire another person to do the crosswalking. They accept that reporting will always be a scramble and some things will fall through the cracks because there just aren't enough hours in the week.

The difference between Heartland before and Heartland after isn't that the work got easier. It's that the system does the tracking so the people can do the work. That's the whole point of custom software in this space. Not replacing judgment. Replacing the stuff that eats the hours judgment needs.
