# LinkedIn Post Series: Heartland for Children

Five posts drawn from an unscripted client interview. Each stands alone. Together they tell the full arc: pain, build, results, trust, and advice.

---

## Post 1: The "Before" Picture

Last week I sat down with a client who told me their old system was "literally standing on one leg."

She wasn't being dramatic. Heartland for Children runs two of the most critical operations in Polk, Highlands, and Hardee counties: a yearly gift-matching event for 1,500 foster kids, and ongoing case tracking for children placed across county and state lines. They serve kids who've been removed from their homes due to abuse, neglect, or abandonment.

For years, both of these operations ran on a Microsoft Access database and a pile of spreadsheets.

Only one person could be in the Access database at a time. It would lock up. Microsoft stopped supporting it. They were at real risk of losing data.

Everything else was manual. Multiple spreadsheets crosswalked against each other. Reporting that was so labor-intensive they just couldn't get to it. Their words: "It was almost like you were busy putting out fires because you really couldn't track."

Here's the thing I keep seeing: organizations in this position don't lack competence. They lack a system that matches the complexity of what they actually do. They're smart people running a sophisticated operation on tools that were never designed for the job.

The question isn't whether the Access database will finally give out. It's what happens between now and then.

Next post: what we built instead, and how the collaboration actually worked.

## Post 2: The Build

After the last post about Heartland for Children's Access database situation, a few people asked what the process actually looks like.

We built two systems for them, about a year apart.

The first was Rudolph Roundup: their annual event matching donors to 1,500 foster children for Christmas gifts. We built a web-based system that captures every child, every donor, and handles the matching. Dashboards show at a glance how many bags are filled, how many are picked up, and what's left to do. No reports required.

The second was their OCS/ICPC system: tracking children placed across county and state lines, home studies in other jurisdictions, case plan assistance for parents in other counties. About 20 different categories of services. Heavy policy and compliance requirements. Thirty-day progress updates. Notifications. Reporting.

Here's what their director of program operations said about working together:

"I felt Mosier Data was very empowered in really seeing the outcomes, like really feeling like they were traveling this with us. Very open to everything that we were saying and needing, really trying to understand it."

We met weekly. Their team tested between meetings. When they changed their mind about something mid-build, it wasn't a big deal. They'd bring it to the table and we'd work through it.

The part that surprised me: by the time we went live, no formal training was needed. Their team had been involved from day one. They learned the system by building it.

Next post: what actually changed once both systems were running.

## Post 3: The Results

Two systems. Two years. Here's what Heartland for Children's director told us changed.

Hard numbers:

- 15% reduction in open services. Once everything was in the system and they could see what was actually assigned, they eliminated the waste.
- Home studies completed in roughly half the time. The system tracks what's happening in other counties and states instead of it getting lost in email threads and spreadsheet tabs.
- Rudolph Roundup would take triple the man hours to run without the system. And the program has grown. More donors. More kids. The system scaled with it.
- Reporting went from "impossible time-wise" to something they do weekly.

Operational side:

Her favorite feature on both systems is the dashboard. Twenty categories of services. At any moment she knows exactly how many they have in each one, how many kids affected, how many families. No reports. No spreadsheets. Just look at the screen.

Her words: "I know the data is very accurate in there."

Most of the organizations I talk to don't need more people. They need their people to stop spending half their week tracking things a system should handle. That's the whole ballgame.

## Post 4: The Second Project

Heartland for Children didn't come back to us because of a sales follow-up sequence.

They came back because the first system worked.

"Because of how well the first database went, how easy it was to work with Mosier, we knew then at that point all the possibilities that we could do with this other very complex process that we had."

The second project was harder. Ongoing case tracking across county and state lines. Policy requirements. Compliance reporting. About 20 categories of services to track. More complex than a once-a-year event by a long shot.

But they'd already seen what custom software could do for Rudolph Roundup. They knew the OCS/ICPC tracking was the more critical operation. They didn't shop around. They just called us.

That's the actual measure of whether the first project went well. Not the testimonial. Not the case study. Whether they bring you the harder problem next.

If you're doing good work, the second project finds you.

## Post 5: For the One on the Fence

At the end of the interview, I asked Heartland for Children's director what she'd say to another organization considering this kind of investment but nervous about pulling the trigger.

"I would tell companies, if they're nervous about this investment, to analyze what their current systems are doing for them and determine: is it time for a change? Is it time for a change to custom build something that could better meet their needs?"

Then she said something I didn't expect:

"Don't be afraid to ask. It can probably be done. I think there were times that things were asked where I sat there and went, oh, I don't know if that could be done, and it could be done. Really, nothing is off the table."

I've been building software for 40 years. The gap between what most people think is possible and what's actually possible with custom software is wider than most folks realize. If you've been patching things together with spreadsheets long enough, you start to believe the constraints are permanent. They're usually not.

Her last line: "Embrace that change."

Pretty good advice, honestly.
