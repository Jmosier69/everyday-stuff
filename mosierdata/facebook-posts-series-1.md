# Facebook Post Series — "What If" Scenarios
## Drafted for Jim Mosier, May 2026

---

### Post 1 — Inventory

Talked to a guy this week who runs a parts supply business. He's tracking inventory across three locations with a spreadsheet and a prayer.

The spreadsheet says he's got 47 of a thing. He walks out back and there are 12. The 47 count includes stuff that's on a truck somewhere, stuff that was sold last week but hasn't been pulled from the sheet yet, and stuff that walked off and nobody knows where it went. His guys have just stopped trusting the numbers. They call each other instead. "Hey, you got any of those brackets?" Every single time.

I asked him how much time that costs him in a week. He just laughed. Not because it was funny.

Who else is running something on a system their people don't actually trust anymore?

---

### Post 2 — Membership organization

There's a membership organization I've been talking to. Hundreds of members, events, dues, committees, the whole thing. Right now it's spread across QuickBooks and Excel and a Gmail account with 47 labels that made sense to someone in 2019.

The part that got me: they're not even sure who's current on dues and who isn't. Someone's been meaning to reconcile it. For six months.

A system that handles member records, renewal reminders, event check-in, and a basic member portal. This isn't exotic. This is table-stakes stuff. It just hasn't been built for them yet.

You know an organization like this. Who comes to mind?

---

### Post 3 — Day spa checkout

I was at a day spa with my wife last weekend. Nice place. The massage was great. Then checkout took eleven minutes.

Eleven. Minutes.

One system for appointments. One for payments. One for the waiver forms. Then my wife and I both have separate membership points. Different accounts, different balances. And none of it can be applied from a single screen. The receptionist is clicking around, re-typing things, apologizing. The couple behind us gave up and sat back down.

I wasn't annoyed. I was taking notes. That receptionist is sharp, she's fast on a keyboard, and the software is what's slow. The business has no idea how much friction they're serving up at the exact moment someone's supposed to walk out feeling great.

You know a place like this. What's the worst checkout or intake process you've sat through lately?

### Post 4 — The "If You Could" question

Here's a question I don't think gets asked enough:

If you could wave a wand and have one part of your business just *work*: no manual steps, no workarounds, no "ask Susan, she knows how to do it." What would it be?

I don't mean buy a new app. I mean really designed around how your people actually operate.

Most business owners I talk to have an answer to this immediately. They've just never been asked.

So I'm asking. What's the thing?

---

### Post 5 — The "Susan" problem

Every business with more than three employees has a Susan.

Susan's the one who knows how to run the report. Susan knows which spreadsheet tab has the real numbers. Susan knows that you have to export it first, then sort by column D, then delete the duplicates, and *then* you can send it to accounting. If Susan leaves, nobody knows how any of this works.

I'm not knocking Susan. Susan's a hero. But if your critical business process lives in someone's head instead of in a system your whole team can use, that's worth fixing.

There's a better way. We should talk.

---

### Post 6 — The spreadsheet ceiling

Spreadsheets are the universal first draft of every business system. I get it. I've built plenty of things that started as "let me just track this in a sheet real quick."

But there's a ceiling. You hit it when:
- More than one person needs to edit at the same time
- You start building "process" around the sheet: "make sure you fill in column G before Friday"
- The sheet becomes the thing you're managing instead of the thing helping you manage

If you're spending more time maintaining the tracker than doing the work the tracker is supposed to support, you're past the ceiling.

What's the tracker you're babysitting right now?

---

### Post 7 — The time-back question

I built a system for Heartland for Children a while back. Case managers were spending hours on paperwork every day. Logging visits, updating files, reconciling records across different systems. It was eating their time and honestly, it was eating at them. These are people who got into the field to help kids, and they were stuck behind a keyboard.

The system we built cut their service times at least in half.

Here's the question I keep coming back to: if you could give your best people half their time back, what would they do with it? More clients served? Deeper work? Going home at a reasonable hour for once?

That's not a hypothetical. For a lot of businesses, it's a few thousand bucks and a couple weeks away.

---

### Post 8 — The "I thought that was normal" moment

Had a client tell me something last year that stuck with me.

We finished building their system and they were using it for about two weeks. I checked in to see how things were going. They said, "You know, I didn't realize how much stress I was carrying just from knowing our old process was fragile. Every time someone ran a report I held my breath."

They'd normalized the anxiety. It was just part of the job. Until it wasn't.

What's something in your business that you've stopped noticing because it's been broken so long?

---

### Post 9 — The cost of not fixing it

People hear "custom software" and think six figures. Sometimes it is. But a lot of what I build is in the low five figures. Sometimes just a few thousand bucks. And it replaces something that's costing way more than that in wasted time.

Real example: a client was paying someone 10 hours a week to manually reconcile data between two systems. Ten hours. Every week. At even a modest hourly rate, that's north of $15,000 a year. We built something that automated it. Paid for itself in under three months.

What's the repetitive thing in your business that you're paying someone to suffer through?

---

### Post 10 — The "I've been meaning to" graveyard

I've heard some version of this from probably fifty different business owners:

"We've been meaning to fix that."

The inventory system they've been meaning to fix. The client onboarding they've been meaning to streamline. The reporting they've been meaning to automate. Two years go by. Three. Nothing changes.

I get why. It's not urgent until it is. Nobody's yelling. The workaround works well enough. Susan hasn't quit yet.

But here's the thing: the stuff you've been meaning to fix is quietly costing you every single day. In time, in mistakes, in good people who get tired of fighting bad systems.

What's the thing you've been meaning to fix for way too long?

---

### Post 11 — If someone handed you a blank slate

Forget what you're using now. Forget the workarounds, the integrations that sort of work, the software you bought three years ago and never fully set up.

If you started from scratch tomorrow. Just you, a developer, and a blank screen. What would you build?

I'm not asking what software you'd buy. I'm asking: what would the perfect version of your business actually look like, from the inside? What would it do? What would your people stop having to do?

Most business owners I ask this have an answer in about ten seconds. They've just never been invited to say it out loud.

So here's the invitation. What would you build?

---

### Post 12 — The phone call test

Here's a quick test to see if your system is actually working:

When a customer calls with a question, how many screens do you have to open to answer it?

One screen? You're in good shape. Two or three? You're normal. Four or more, plus "let me call you back after I track that down"? You're burning time and trust every single time the phone rings.

I've watched receptionists toggle between five different tabs just to tell a customer when their order might ship. That's not a receptionist problem. That's a system problem.

What's your number? Be honest.
